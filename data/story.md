## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 19.** DR Congo stands at **2,344 confirmed cases and 930 deaths**. Add Uganda and France and the global count is 2,365 confirmed and 932 deaths. The crude case fatality ratio is **39.7 percent** in DRC and climbing toward 40.

WHO published [Disease Outbreak News 613](https://www.who.int/emergencies/disease-outbreak-news/item/2026-DON613) on July 17, with data as of July 15. It is the first reconciled WHO figure since Sitrep 09 and it puts the DRC CFR at 39 percent flat. The detail underneath is where the outbreak's shape shows. Cases have reached 46 health zones across five provinces, and 38 of those zones reported a case in the past 21 days. In that same 21 days the outbreak added 969 confirmed cases and 524 confirmed deaths. Health workers account for 119 infections and 36 deaths, a fatality ratio of 30 percent inside the response itself.

That last number is the one to sit with. The people running the response are dying at close to the rate of the population they are treating. DON613 names why: occupational exposure, infection prevention and control that is not holding in health facilities, and exposure in the community on top of it. A response that cannot protect its own staff loses capacity exactly when it needs to add it.

Reporting went quiet and then came back in a rush. BNO published no daily graphic between July 14 and July 19, while the Congo health ministry ran late. Reuters carried the ministry's situation reports through the gap, at 2,181 cases and 864 deaths, then 2,267 and 893. Those figures chain onto each other exactly, and BNO's July 19 graphic chains onto them again, so the week reconstructs cleanly rather than by interpolation. July 16 and July 20 stay flat or empty because nothing was published for them.

Uganda is on the clock. Its last patient was discharged July 16 after two negative tests, starting the 42-day countdown to an end-of-outbreak declaration. Uganda holds at 20 confirmed cases and two deaths. WHO tracks one additional probable case and death that this tracker keeps out of the confirmed columns.

Sitrep 10 was nominally due July 19 and has not appeared. Sitrep 09, data as of July 12, is still the last weekly reconciliation, so every bar after July 12 stays hatched. The gap between the reconciled WHO figure (2,145 global confirmed as of July 15) and the running wire count (2,365 as of July 19) is the four days of surveillance that have not yet been checked.
