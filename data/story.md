## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 14.** The [BNO News daily graphic for 14 June](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2066315971992617109), sourced to the DRC and Uganda health ministries and WHO, puts DRC at **782 confirmed cases and 181 deaths** and Uganda unchanged at **19 confirmed and 2 deaths**. Suspected cases hold at 116. **Global: 801 confirmed, 116 suspected, 183 confirmed deaths.** Thirty days since the first case.

The day-over-day change is the largest of the outbreak so far: **plus 72 confirmed cases and plus 32 deaths in 24 hours**. The prior single-day record was plus 48 confirmed, set on 9 June. DRC's case fatality rate on the running count now sits near 23%, well above the roughly 15% the last WHO Sitrep reconciled for the province-weighted total. A surge that big in one day usually means a batch of backlogged samples cleared the lab at once, so part of it is dates catching up rather than one day of real transmission. The split won't be clear until WHO reconciles it.

No new weekly Sitrep landed this week. [Sitrep 04](https://www.afro.who.int/countries/democratic-republic-of-congo/publications), data as of 7 June, is still the last reconciled point, at DRC 550 confirmed and 101 deaths. So the gap between the daily wire and the last WHO reconciliation is now seven days wide and 232 confirmed cases deep. That gap is the thing to watch. The last two reconciliations came in above the wire, not below, because backlog confirmations get attributed back to the dates the infections belong to. So the next Sitrep could push these June numbers up rather than down.

The structure underneath has not moved. Bundibugyo still has no approved vaccine or treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. North Kivu still carries a case fatality rate near 60%, more than three times the rate in Ituri, and Ituri still carries the bulk of DRC's cases. Response operations still run against armed conflict, security incidents at health facilities, and community resistance. CDC holds DRC at a Level 3 travel notice and Uganda at Level 2. The charts treat bars through 7 June as Sitrep-stable; 8 June onward stays provisional on the daily wire.
