## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**May 31.** WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus continues his visit to Bunia, the epicenter of the outbreak in Ituri Province. At a [press briefing on 30 May](https://www.who.int/news-room/speeches/item/who-director-general-s-remarks-at-the-press-briefing-on-the-on-the-bundibugyo-ebola-outbreak---30-may-2026), he reframed the response around community ownership: "We are not here to tell people what to do. We are here to listen. Communities understand their own challenges and their own solutions. Our role is to support you in implementing those solutions, together." He asked countries with travel bans or border closures to reconsider: "These measures make the response harder, and they discourage the transparency that saves lives."

On 30 May, the DRC Ministry of Health [removed nearly 700 suspected cases](https://x.com/BNOFeed) from its running count after lab testing returned negative results, reflecting an expanded testing pipeline rather than a real-world recovery. BNO News's @BNOFeed Daily Ebola Update graphic for 30 May reports a cleaned-baseline DRC running count of 574 cases and 17 deaths, with Uganda unchanged at 9 confirmed and 1 death. On 31 May, BNO reports DRC running at 672 cases (+98) and 55 deaths (+38) on the cleaned baseline. WHO has not reconciled the reduction; per the tracker's no-dip rule, the WHO DON605 figures (**906 suspected, 134 confirmed, 223 suspected deaths**) carry forward until a second high-value source corroborates the dip. WHO Sitrep 03 is expected Tuesday 2 June and will likely catch up on both the +100 lab confirmations on 29 May and the ~700-case removal on 30 May.

The DRC MoH cycle (suspected surge → lab catch-up → reclassification down) is the surveillance signal working as intended, but it makes a chart that looks like the outbreak is reversing. It isn't. Confirmed cases continue to rise.

The Bundibugyo virus has no approved treatment or vaccine. Response operations face armed conflict in Ituri (ADF attacks; M23 controls Goma and Bukavu in the affected Kivu provinces, with rebel-reported cases there), community resistance over burial protocols, and at least three attacks on health centers. EU medical aid reached Ituri on 28 May with more shipments arriving over the following eight days; the US has committed more than $112 million to the response. Doctors Without Borders [warned on 30 May](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/who-chief-visits-congo-ebola-epicenter-as-cases-outpace-response) that the response is failing to keep pace: "Never before has an Ebola outbreak recorded so many cases so soon after its declaration. Nobody knows the true scale and severity of this outbreak," said Dr. Alan Gonzalez, MSF's deputy director of operations. A Kenyan court [blocked the US plan](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/kenyan-court-blocks-u-s-plan-to-open-ebola-quarantine-center-to-treat-americansebola-outbreak) to open a 50-bed quarantine center in Kenya for Americans exposed to Ebola.
