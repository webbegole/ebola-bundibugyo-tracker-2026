## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 16.** WHO put the DRC outbreak at **2,073 confirmed cases and 796 deaths** at a Geneva media briefing. Director-General Tedros called it the third-largest Ebola outbreak on record, and in the past month the fastest-spreading of any on record. The 2018-2019 outbreak in the same country took more than ten months to reach 2,000 cases. This one did it in two.

The case fatality ratio sits near **38 percent** and keeps climbing. Tedros named the reason. More than 80 percent of new cases turn up outside known contact lists, so the transmission chains are being missed. About two-thirds of deaths happen in communities, in people who never reach a health facility. WHO emergencies chief Chikwe Ihekweazu put numbers on the gap: come into care early and the fatality ratio runs 10 to 15 percent, die in the community and it runs 60 to 70. The ones being counted are the ones found too late.

The response is losing ground to conflict, not only to the virus. A treatment center in Bunia was attacked on Wednesday. WHO and Africa CDC are still short more than $400 million on the joint response plan, and UNICEF says it holds a quarter of what it needs. Treatment capacity has grown past 800 beds, labs from one to 16, contact follow-up to almost 80 percent. 377 people have recovered.

Uganda reached a milestone. Its last confirmed patient was discharged on July 16, starting the 42-day countdown to declaring that outbreak over. Uganda holds at 20 cases and two deaths, 15 imported from DRC and five from local transmission.

CDC caught up to the geography. On July 15 it re-scoped its Level 3 travel notice to name four provinces, adding Haut-Uele and Tshopo to Ituri and Nord-Kivu. Tshopo's capital is Kisangani, one of the largest cities in the country. Haut-Uele borders South Sudan and the Central African Republic.

The 2,073 figure comes from a WHO briefing, not a Weekly Sitrep, so it carries no explicit "as of" date. The last reconciled Sitrep is still [number 09](https://www.afro.who.int/countries/democratic-republic-of-congo/publication/ebola-bundibugyo-virus-disease-outbreak-2), data as of 12 July, at 1,984 confirmed and 721 deaths. Everything after 12 July stays provisional on the charts until Sitrep 10 reconciles it. BNO's daily graphic has gone quiet since July 14 while the Congo health ministry delays its updates, so July 15 and 17 sit as gaps rather than guesses.
