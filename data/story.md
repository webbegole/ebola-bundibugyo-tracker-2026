## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 16.** WHO published [Weekly External Situation Report 05](https://www.afro.who.int/countries/democratic-republic-of-congo/publications), data as of 14 June. It is the first reconciled WHO data point since Sitrep 04 on 7 June, and it closes an eight-day gap. WHO puts DRC at **808 confirmed cases and 192 confirmed deaths**, a case fatality rate of 23.8%. Uganda holds at **19 confirmed and 2 confirmed deaths**, plus one probable case and one probable death, for a Uganda total of 20 cases and 3 deaths. **Global confirmed: 827 cases, 194 deaths.** Suspected cases hold at 116.

The Sitrep reconciled the 14 June figure upward. The tracker had carried 14 June at 801 confirmed and 183 deaths from the daily wire. WHO put it at 827 and 194. That is the same direction the last two reconciliations showed. Backlog confirmations get attributed back to the dates the infections belong to, so WHO comes in above the same-day wire, not below. The reconciled 14 June count equals the BNO running count for 15 June, which flattens the apparent one-day jump. The most recent daily graphic, [BNO News for 15 June](https://x.com/BNOFeed), holds DRC at 808 and 192, the same place WHO now puts 14 June.

The warnings sharpened this week. The head of Africa CDC said the outbreak could be the worst Ebola event yet, with tens of thousands of contacts still untraced. The Red Cross told [Insider Paper](https://x.com/TheInsiderPaper) the peak is still "in front of us" and the response could run a year. African Union heads of state met on the outbreak, with President Ramaphosa leading as the AU's pandemic-preparedness champion. The BBC filmed survivors recovering from the Bundibugyo strain in one of Ituri's worst-hit zones, the first sustained good news from the response.

The structure underneath has not moved. Bundibugyo still has no approved vaccine or treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. North Kivu carried a case fatality rate near 60% in the last province breakdown WHO published, more than three times the rate in Ituri, and Ituri still carries the bulk of DRC's cases. Response operations still run against armed conflict, security incidents at health facilities, and community resistance. CDC's travel notices are unchanged, with DRC's affected provinces at Level 3 and a combined DRC and Uganda Level 2 notice covering the rest. The charts now treat bars through 14 June as Sitrep-stable; only 15 June stays provisional on the daily wire.
