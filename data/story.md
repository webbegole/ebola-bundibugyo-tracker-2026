## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 12.** The outbreak stands at **1,947 confirmed cases and 704 confirmed deaths**. DRC carries almost all of it, 1,926 cases and 702 deaths, a case fatality ratio of about **36 percent**. The [12 July figure](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2076464126495179108) added 53 cases and 30 deaths. Uganda holds at 20 cases and 2 deaths, no new case since 21 June. France stays at its single imported case, recovered. Reuters, citing the DRC's national public health institute, reports the same 1,926 and 702, so two independent sources carry this count.

The outbreak now has five affected provinces instead of three. The DRC's public health institute [designated Haut-Uele and Tshopo as epidemic zones](https://www.cnbcafrica.com/2026/congo-says-ebola-outbreak-spreads-to-provinces-haut-uele-and-tshopo): four cases including two deaths in Tshopo, one death in Haut-Uele. Investigators trace all of them back to Nia Nia health zone in Ituri, so this is exported transmission rather than a new focus. The geography still matters. Tshopo's capital is Kisangani, one of the largest cities in the country, and Haut-Uele borders South Sudan and the Central African Republic. A chain that reaches a city of that size, or a border, changes what containment has to cover.

The mechanism behind the rising fatality ratio has not changed. Bundibugyo has no approved vaccine and no approved treatment, so the ceiling is set entirely by how fast cases get found and isolated. DRC's case fatality has climbed from about 29 percent on 28 June to about 36 percent on 12 July, deaths growing faster than confirmed cases for two weeks straight. That pattern is what a detection problem looks like: the people being counted are the ones already sick enough to die.

Misinformation is now part of the transmission system. In Bunia, four Red Cross volunteers were injured when a crowd tried to open a coffin during a safe burial, convinced it was empty and that Ebola is not real. BBC Verify counted 12 separate cases of community resistance to control measures. Rumours delay care, and delayed care spreads the virus.

WHO's last reconciled figure remains [Weekly Situation Report 08](https://www.afro.who.int/health-topics/ebola-disease/outbreak-drc-26), data as of 5 July: 1,645 confirmed cases, 523 confirmed deaths, a confirmed case fatality ratio of 31.8 percent, and 12,417 contacts still to follow. Bars through 5 July render solid on the charts. Everything after 5 July stays provisional.
