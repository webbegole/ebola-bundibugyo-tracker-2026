## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 7.** WHO has gone quiet. No Disease Outbreak News, no Weekly Sitrep, no Geneva briefing since the 5 June snapshot. So the freshest number now comes from the wire. The [BNO News daily graphic for 7 June](https://x.com/BNOFeed), sourced to the DRC and Uganda health ministries and WHO, puts DRC at **515 confirmed cases and 91 deaths** and Uganda unchanged at **19 confirmed and 2 deaths**. Suspected cases hold at 116. **Global: 534 confirmed, 116 suspected, 93 confirmed deaths.** Read these as provisional. The same wire said 507 cases on 6 June and 471 on 5 June, so the line is still climbing.

One caveat does the work here. The BNO running count is a DRC ministry tally that WHO has not yet reconciled, and every time WHO has caught up it has come in lower. On 1 June the wire said 352 confirmed and WHO reconciled it to 330. On 5 June the wire said 471 and WHO landed at 400. That is a 15 to 18% overshoot each time. The tracker normally waits for WHO and treats the wire as a lead, but with WHO silent for three days the choice is a stale 5 June number or the ministry figure with a flag on it. I took the ministry figure and flagged it. When WHO publishes, expect the confirmed count to settle below 534, and expect the no-dip rule to need the usual two-source reconciliation before the tracker follows it down.

The structure of the outbreak has not moved. Bundibugyo still has no approved vaccine or treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. That is the whole mechanism in the CDC model from 5 June: at 20% of cases isolated within two days the outbreak can rival the 2014-2016 West Africa epidemic, and at 70% there is a 94% chance of holding it under 10,000. DRC reported contact tracing around 55% at last count, short of the 90% target. Response operations still run against armed conflict in Ituri, community resistance over burials, and attacks on health centers. The US entry restrictions that DRC's health minister called discriminatory remain in place. No WHO Weekly Sitrep 03 has published; when it lands it will be the first full reconciliation of the post-5-June surge.
