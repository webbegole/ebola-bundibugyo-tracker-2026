## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 3.** WHO published [Disease Outbreak News DON612](https://www.who.int/emergencies/disease-outbreak-news/item/2026-DON612) on 3 July, its first reconciled update in two weeks. It puts the outbreak at **1,481 confirmed cases and 454 confirmed deaths** as of 1 July for DRC and 2 July for Uganda. DRC carries 1,460 cases and 452 deaths, a case fatality ratio of **30.9 percent**. Uganda holds at 20 confirmed cases and 2 deaths, with one probable death on top, and has reported no new case since 21 June. France stays at its single imported case.

The DON revises our 2 July death count up from 449 to 454. WHO counts five more deaths than the wire did for the same case total, the usual direction. Reconciliation catches deaths the running count missed. The DON also widens the map. Three confirmed cases carried the virus out of Nia Nia health zone in Ituri to Wamba in Haut-Uele and to Kisangani in Tshopo, the first cases outside the original three provinces. WHO now counts 36 affected health zones, 21 of them still active. Health and care workers account for 102 cases and 25 deaths.

The [BNO graphic for 3 July](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2073152512186536401) runs ahead of the DON, at **1,523 confirmed and 475 deaths**. DRC drives all of it, at 1,502 cases and 473 deaths, a case fatality ratio of about **31.5 percent** and a new high. The structure has not changed. Bundibugyo still has no approved vaccine or treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. A WHO-sponsored trial of two antivirals, MBP134 and remdesivir, has now begun in Ituri (Health Policy Watch, 4 July), the first attempt to put a specific treatment against this virus.

The charts treat bars through 28 June as Sitrep-stable. DON612 is a Disease Outbreak News, not a Weekly Sitrep, so the hatching boundary stays at 28 June and the 1 July through 3 July bars stay provisional on the daily wire.
