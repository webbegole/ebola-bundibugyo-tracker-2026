## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 14.** WHO published [Weekly Situation Report 09](https://www.afro.who.int/countries/democratic-republic-of-congo/publication/ebola-bundibugyo-virus-disease-outbreak-2), data as of 12 July. It reconciles the outbreak to **1,984 confirmed cases and 721 confirmed deaths**. DRC carries almost all of it, 1,963 cases and 719 deaths, a case fatality ratio of **36.6 percent**. Uganda holds at 20 cases and 2 deaths, plus one probable case who died, no new confirmed case since 21 June. France stays at its single imported case, recovered.

This is the gold-standard source, so it outranks the daily wire count. The Sitrep pulls the 12 July figure up from the 1,947 and 704 the BNO graphic carried that day. The [13 July graphic](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2077041388084936939) then re-published the exact Sitrep numbers, 1,963 and 719 for DRC, so 12 and 13 July sit flat at the reconciled count. No new BNO update has posted for 14 July yet.

Read week over week, the Sitrep is a bad number. Since [Report 08](https://www.afro.who.int/health-topics/ebola-disease/outbreak-drc-26) on 5 July, DRC added 339 confirmed cases and 198 confirmed deaths. Deaths grew faster than cases, so the confirmed fatality ratio moved from 31.8 percent to 36.6 percent in one week. WHO fits a log-linear model to daily incidence and reads an average daily growth of 2.3 percent. The outbreak is still expanding, not plateauing.

The mechanism behind the rising fatality ratio has not changed. Bundibugyo has no approved vaccine and no approved treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. A fatality ratio climbing while cases climb is what a detection problem looks like: the people being counted are the ones already sick enough to die.

The geography kept widening. WHO now counts five affected provinces and 42 health zones, 33 of them with active transmission, up from 36 zones a week earlier. Wamba in Haut-Uele, Ariwara and Boga in Ituri, and three zones in Tshopo reported their first confirmed cases. Tshopo's capital is Kisangani, one of the largest cities in the country, and Haut-Uele borders South Sudan and the Central African Republic. CDC has not yet re-scoped its Level 3 travel notice, which still names only the original three provinces.

Bars through 12 July now render solid on the charts. Everything after 12 July stays provisional until the next Sitrep reconciles it.
