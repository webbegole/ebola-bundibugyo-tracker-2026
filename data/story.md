## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 1.** The lab-catch-up cycle is now visible in the structure of the data. BNO's [Daily Ebola Update graphic for June 1](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2061573338569351279), sourced to MoH DR Congo, MoH Uganda, and WHO, splits DRC's case count for the first time on the cleaned baseline: **343 confirmed, 116 suspected, 48 deaths in DRC; 9 confirmed, 1 death in Uganda; 352 confirmed and 49 deaths globally**. Suspected cases dropped another 293 in DRC overnight (-71%). Confirmed cases rose 80. BNO published a follow-up note explaining the structural shift: "Because the number of suspected cases is constantly changing, and not always available, our updates will now focus on confirmed cases. The number of suspected cases is still shown in the graphic."

That sentence describes the surveillance mechanism that has been running since the May 30 baseline reset. Suspected cases were set wide so nothing slips through. Lab capacity catches up. Negatives get removed. The suspected-cases bar narrows, and the confirmed-cases bar widens. The tracker also corrects yesterday's row: DRC's confirmed deaths revised from 55 to 42, matching DRC MoH's 31 May situation report (282 confirmed / 42 confirmed deaths / 220 suspected) and the implied "yesterday" baseline on today's BNO graphic. The earlier 55 was an inference; the formal MoH figure is 42.

WHO's [update on Sunday](https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/06/1167613), carried by UN News June 1, gives a more conservative count: 210 confirmed in DRC, 17 confirmed deaths, around 350 suspected under investigation, and 16 health workers infected. Five people have now recovered: four nurses discharged from hospital, plus a lab worker who cleared earlier in the week. WHO has handed over a 24-bed refurbished Ebola Treatment Centre in Bunia to DRC's health authorities, with a 60-bed total capacity, and is setting up a 42-bed annexe. The gap between WHO's 210 and DRC MoH's 282 confirmed reflects WHO's partial reconciliation of the lab catch-up; full reconciliation arrives Tuesday June 2 with WHO Sitrep 03.

The DRC MoH cycle (suspected surge → lab catch-up → reclassification down) is the surveillance signal working as intended. The visual drop on the chart is that mechanism running for the second day in a row. Confirmed cases continue to rise.

The Bundibugyo virus has no approved treatment or vaccine. Response operations face armed conflict in Ituri (ADF attacks; M23 controls Goma and Bukavu in the affected Kivu provinces, with rebel-reported cases there), community resistance over burial protocols, and at least three attacks on health centers. EU medical aid reached Ituri on 28 May with more shipments arriving over the following eight days; the US has committed more than $112 million to the response. Doctors Without Borders [warned on 30 May](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/who-chief-visits-congo-ebola-epicenter-as-cases-outpace-response) that the response is failing to keep pace: "Never before has an Ebola outbreak recorded so many cases so soon after its declaration. Nobody knows the true scale and severity of this outbreak," said Dr. Alan Gonzalez, MSF's deputy director of operations. A Kenyan court [blocked the US plan](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/kenyan-court-blocks-u-s-plan-to-open-ebola-quarantine-center-to-treat-americansebola-outbreak) to open a 50-bed quarantine center in Kenya for Americans exposed to Ebola. The Government of the DRC and WHO closed their joint Bunia mission on 31 May with a [joint statement](https://www.who.int/news/item/31-05-2026-joint-statement-by-the-government-of-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-who-concerning-the-outbreak-of-ebola-disease-caused-by-the-bundibugyo-virus) committing to "rapidly undertake randomized control trials on candidate vaccines and treatments" and asking other governments to keep "borders remain open" so medical supplies and personnel can move.
