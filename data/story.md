## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**May 31.** The cumulative chart drops sharply on May 30 today. That is not the outbreak reversing. It is a definitional cleanup by DRC's Ministry of Health, which announced on May 30 that it had removed nearly 700 suspected cases from its running count after lab testing returned negative for Ebola. DRC's testing pipeline ramped up over the prior week. The backlog cleared. The remaining suspected cases get a tighter denominator, and the suspected-deaths column gets zeroed in the same pass. The tracker applies the cleaned baseline today under a methodology-change carve-out documented in METHODOLOGY.md: when the official surveillance source formally announces a definitional reclassification, the no-dip rule's two-source threshold is waived. The new figures are **349 suspected, 234 confirmed, 583 total cases, 0 suspected deaths** as of May 30, with the suspected-cases count climbing to 447 and total to 681 on May 31 as new cases layer onto the cleaner baseline. Confirmed deaths in DRC now sit at 55, reflecting DRC MoH's continued lab confirmation of previously probable deaths. WHO Sitrep 03 is expected Tuesday June 2 and will reconcile.

Same day, the Government of the DRC and WHO closed the Tedros / Kamba / Muyaya joint mission to Bunia with a [joint statement](https://www.who.int/news/item/31-05-2026-joint-statement-by-the-government-of-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-who-concerning-the-outbreak-of-ebola-disease-caused-by-the-bundibugyo-virus). The statement is qualitative and does three things at once. It commits both parties to "rapidly undertake randomized control trials on candidate vaccines and treatments" against the Bundibugyo strain. It puts community trust at the center of the response: "Success will depend on the trust, engagement and leadership of local communities." And it makes an explicit ask of other governments, formalizing Tedros's 30 May press-briefing pushback: "Cooperation between countries must also ensure that borders remain open, and that entry controls do not obstruct the flow of desperately needed medical supplies and personnel."

The mission also opened a new 42-bed Ebola treatment center in Bunia — a pediatric building at the Centre Évangélique Médical converted to outbreak duty and entrusted to WHO. It joins the rehabilitated 60-bed Bunia facility WHO announced on 26 May.

The DRC MoH cycle (suspected surge → lab catch-up → reclassification down) is the surveillance signal working as intended. Suspected cases get a clinical-suspicion bar set deliberately wide so nothing slips through. Lab capacity catches up. Negatives get removed. What's left is a tighter, more confident denominator. The visual drop on the chart is that mechanism running for the first time on this outbreak. Confirmed cases continue to rise.

The Bundibugyo virus has no approved treatment or vaccine. Response operations face armed conflict in Ituri (ADF attacks; M23 controls Goma and Bukavu in the affected Kivu provinces, with rebel-reported cases there), community resistance over burial protocols, and at least three attacks on health centers. EU medical aid reached Ituri on 28 May with more shipments arriving over the following eight days; the US has committed more than $112 million to the response. Doctors Without Borders [warned on 30 May](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/who-chief-visits-congo-ebola-epicenter-as-cases-outpace-response) that the response is failing to keep pace: "Never before has an Ebola outbreak recorded so many cases so soon after its declaration. Nobody knows the true scale and severity of this outbreak," said Dr. Alan Gonzalez, MSF's deputy director of operations. A Kenyan court [blocked the US plan](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/kenyan-court-blocks-u-s-plan-to-open-ebola-quarantine-center-to-treat-americansebola-outbreak) to open a 50-bed quarantine center in Kenya for Americans exposed to Ebola.
