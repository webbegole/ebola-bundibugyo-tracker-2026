## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 5.** The confirmed count keeps climbing as the labs catch up. DRC stands at **381 confirmed cases and 64 deaths**, with 233 people hospitalized in isolation, per the [WHO Disease Outbreak News](https://www.who.int/emergencies/disease-outbreak-news) snapshot as of 5 June and DRC Health Minister Roger Kamba's [4 June briefing](https://english.news.cn/africa/20260605/75381cda9c1642f7acfdaac060a5bf0f/c.html). The cases sit across three provinces: 359 in Ituri across 17 health zones, 19 in North Kivu across seven, and 3 in South Kivu. Uganda has risen to **19 confirmed and 2 deaths**, its second death and three new cases since the start of the month, all contacts of known cases. Suspected cases hold at 116. **Global: 400 confirmed, 116 suspected, 66 confirmed deaths.**

Kamba pointed to the part of the response that is working. Testing capacity jumped after more than 4,000 kits arrived from Africa CDC, and the labs now turn nearly every sample around within 24 hours. Contact tracing climbed from about 9% at the start to 55%, still short of the 90% that would let the response get ahead of the virus. BNO's June 5 wire graphic runs well ahead of the reconciled count at 471 confirmed and 84 deaths, the same forward-lead pattern seen on June 1 and June 3. The tracker follows the WHO figures and notes the gap; the DRC ministry's daily running count keeps landing in a WHO publication a day or two later.

The CDC put numbers to the stakes. It released [three reports on 5 June](https://www.cdc.gov/media/releases/2026/update-on-ebola-outbreak-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-6-5-2026.html): a field report calling this the largest Bundibugyo outbreak on record, a risk assessment that keeps the risk to the US public low, and a modeling analysis from its Center for Forecasting and Outbreak Analytics. The model is a planning tool, not a forecast. It says that without strong intervention the outbreak could rival or exceed the 2014-2016 West Africa epidemic, with more than 20,000 cases in two of three simulations when only 20% of cases are isolated within two days of symptoms. Push isolation to 70% and there is a 94% chance of holding the outbreak under 10,000. The mechanism is the whole story: speed of detection and isolation sets the ceiling.

The Bundibugyo virus still has no approved treatment or vaccine. WHO and Africa CDC have launched a joint response plan; clinical trials of candidate therapeutics and vaccines are being prioritized. Response operations still face armed conflict in Ituri (ADF attacks; M23 controls Goma and Bukavu in the affected Kivu provinces), community resistance over burial protocols, and attacks on health centers. The US has imposed entry restrictions and airport screening that DRC's health minister called discriminatory. On 31 May the Government of the DRC and WHO closed their joint Bunia mission with a [joint statement](https://www.who.int/news/item/31-05-2026-joint-statement-by-the-government-of-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-who-concerning-the-outbreak-of-ebola-disease-caused-by-the-bundibugyo-virus) asking other governments to keep borders open. No WHO Weekly Sitrep 03 has published yet; the Disease Outbreak News and media briefings remain the reconciliation channel.
