## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 23.** The outbreak reached Europe. France [reported its first Ebola case](https://news.sky.com/story/france-reports-first-ebola-case-after-doctor-returning-from-dr-congo-tests-positive-13556940), a doctor who tested positive after returning from a humanitarian mission in DRC. The patient is isolated and in stable condition, and ECDC put the risk to the wider European public as low. This is an imported case, not local transmission, so the BNO and WHO outbreak counts do not fold it into the running total, and the global figures here stay DRC plus Uganda. The case still matters. It is the first time this Bundibugyo outbreak has surfaced outside Africa, and it is the kind of single traveler that border screening is built to catch.

The African count kept climbing. The daily [BNO News graphic for 23 June](https://x.com/BNOFeed), sourced to the DRC and Uganda health ministries and WHO, puts DRC at **1,094 confirmed cases and 277 confirmed deaths**. Uganda moved for the first time in weeks, ticking from 19 to **20 confirmed cases**, with 2 confirmed deaths. **Global confirmed: 1,114 cases, 279 deaths.** Suspected cases hold at 116. Over the trailing seven days the count added 258 confirmed cases and 81 confirmed deaths.

The structure underneath has not moved. Bundibugyo still has no approved vaccine or treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. The Uganda tick is worth watching after more than two weeks flat, though one case is not a trend yet. There is no new WHO reconciliation behind these rows. The last one is [Weekly Situation Report 05](https://www.afro.who.int/countries/democratic-republic-of-congo/publications), data as of 14 June, which put global confirmed at 827. CDC's travel notices are unchanged, with DRC's affected provinces at Level 3 and a combined DRC and Uganda Level 2 notice covering the rest. The charts treat bars through 14 June as Sitrep-stable; 15 through 23 June stay provisional on the daily wire.
