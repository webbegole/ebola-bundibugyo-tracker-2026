## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 22.** DR Congo now carries **2,536 confirmed cases and 1,033 deaths** on its own. Yesterday it was one death short of a thousand. Today it is past it, and the global count sits at 2,557 confirmed and **1,035 deaths**. So DRC crossed the line by itself, without leaning on the two deaths in Uganda that pushed the global figure over yesterday.

Look at how fast this got here. The 2013 to 2016 West Africa outbreak is still the worst on record, more than 11,000 dead. It took about eight months to reach its first thousand deaths. This outbreak reached a thousand in roughly ten weeks. The BNO graphic marks it as day 68 since the first case. That is the mechanism worth naming: not the total, which is still a fraction of West Africa, but the slope.

The crude case fatality ratio in DRC is **40.7 percent**, globally 40.5. Both climbed past 40 this week and kept going. Roughly four in ten confirmed cases end in a recorded death, and that is before counting whatever surveillance has not reached.

The number is well sourced today. The [BNO daily graphic](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2080108020382462380), embedded-dated July 22, gives 2,536 for DRC and 1,033 deaths, +63 and +34 on yesterday. [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/22/nearly-1000-people-have-died-from-ebola-in-dr-congo), carrying AFP, AP and Reuters, still shows the prior day's DRC report of 2,473 and 999, which is the figure this row supersedes. NPR relayed the thousand-death crossing on the wire. The counts line up.

Two people who run the response said the quiet part out loud this week. Africa CDC's Jean Kaseya called the outbreak "escalating at an alarming pace" and warned that if it is not stopped now it will become "the worst outbreak the world has ever documented." WHO's incident manager for Bundibugyo, Thierno Balde, put it plainer: "the outbreak remains ahead of us and we are still in a phase of catching up."

Sitrep 10 was due July 19. It is now four days past and still has not appeared. Sitrep 09, data as of July 12, remains the last weekly reconciliation, so every bar after July 12 stays hatched. The gap between the last WHO-reconciled figure (2,145 global confirmed as of July 15) and today's running count (2,557) is more than 400 cases nobody has reconciled yet. Historically that gap closes upward.

Uganda is still the one thing moving the other way. The 42-day countdown WHO AFRO started on July 16 is at day six, and Uganda holds at 20 confirmed cases and two deaths. Kaseya has written to the US asking it to lift Uganda travel restrictions on the strength of that month without a new case. The clock resets to zero if a single new imported case turns up, and DRC transmission has not slowed.
