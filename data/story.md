## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 3.** Tedros briefed the media in Geneva a day after returning from the Ituri epicentre. DRC now stands at **344 confirmed cases and 60 deaths across 24 health zones** in Ituri, North Kivu and South Kivu, per the [WHO Director-General's opening remarks](https://www.who.int/news-room/speeches/item/who-director-general-s-opening-remarks-at-the-media-briefing---3-june-2026). Suspected cases hold at 116, down from over 1,000 last week, as the lab backlog clears and cases are either confirmed or ruled out. Uganda is unchanged at 15 confirmed and 1 death. One of the Uganda cases is a Congolese resident who travelled to the United Arab Emirates and then to Uganda; WHO is working with both governments on contact tracing. A US citizen infected in DRC is still in care in Germany. **Global: 359 confirmed, 116 suspected, 61 confirmed deaths.**

The confirmed count rose 23 in a day (DRC 321 → 344) and confirmed deaths rose 12 (48 → 60), the lab system catching up rather than a fresh surge. Six people have recovered in DRC and two in Uganda. WHO's risk assessment is unchanged: very high nationally, high regionally, low globally. BNO's June 3 wire graphic runs a step ahead at 378 cases / 63 deaths; the tracker follows the WHO-reconciled figures and notes the gap, the same overshoot pattern seen on June 1.

Tedros named the binding constraints. Contact tracing in DRC sits near **45%** and needs to clear 90% to get ahead of transmission, held back by insecurity, displacement and population movement. Testing capacity is being decentralized to Mongbwalu, Beni, Aru, Nyakunde and Tchomia to cut confirmation delays. Bunia now runs three treatment centres with 80 beds, with units also in Mongbwalu, Rwampara, Beni, Goma and Bukavu. He asked the countries that imposed blanket travel restrictions to lift them, calling them a drag on supply chains and the response, and pointed to community mistrust as a serious barrier, with some local leaders telling him they do not believe Ebola is real.

The Bundibugyo virus still has no approved treatment or vaccine; WHO convened its interim Medical Countermeasures Network a second time to push decentralized diagnostics, country-led clinical trials, and response funding. Tedros framed the real test as what comes after: DRC has stopped 16 previous Ebola outbreaks, and the question is how fast this one ends and whether the health system left behind can handle malaria, malnutrition and the other threats these communities face. Response operations still face armed conflict in Ituri (ADF attacks; M23 controls Goma and Bukavu in the affected Kivu provinces), community resistance over burial protocols, and attacks on health centers. The US has committed more than $112 million; a Kenyan court [blocked the US plan](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/kenyan-court-blocks-u-s-plan-to-open-ebola-quarantine-center-to-treat-americansebola-outbreak) to open a 50-bed quarantine center. On 31 May the Government of the DRC and WHO closed their joint Bunia mission with a [joint statement](https://www.who.int/news/item/31-05-2026-joint-statement-by-the-government-of-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-who-concerning-the-outbreak-of-ebola-disease-caused-by-the-bundibugyo-virus) asking other governments to keep borders open. No WHO Weekly Sitrep 03 has published yet; the media briefings remain the reconciliation channel.
