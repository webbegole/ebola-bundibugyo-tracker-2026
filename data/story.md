## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 7.** DRC's health ministry, running days behind after a reporting delay, released its latest count: the outbreak stands at **1,729 confirmed cases and 582 confirmed deaths**. DRC carries almost all of it, 1,708 cases and 580 deaths, a case fatality ratio near **34 percent**. The [7 July figure](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2074791362377969887) added 84 cases and 59 deaths in a day. That 59 is the largest single-day rise in deaths of the outbreak so far, and it lands alongside a health-worker strike in Ituri over unpaid benefits and a backlog at the Congo ministry. Uganda holds at 20 cases and 2 deaths, no new case since 21 June. France stays at its single imported case, recovered.

The scale is now on the record. STAT's Helen Branswell notes this is already the third largest Ebola outbreak ever recorded, and at 1,708 DRC cases it sits about halfway to the second largest, the 2018 to 2020 DRC epidemic at 3,470 cases. The DRC INSP situation report n053 that carried the 7 July count also put 12,190 contacts on the monitoring list, with roughly three in four reached that day.

Watch the ratio, not the count. DRC's case fatality has climbed from about 29 percent on 28 June to about 34 percent on 7 July, deaths growing faster than confirmed cases. The structure has not changed. Bundibugyo still has no approved vaccine or treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. The WHO-sponsored trial of two antivirals, MBP134 and remdesivir, is running in Ituri, the first specific treatment tried against this virus.

The last reconciled WHO figures remain [DON612](https://www.who.int/emergencies/disease-outbreak-news/item/2026-DON612) from 3 July, at 1,481 cases and 454 deaths, and Weekly Sitrep 07 as of 28 June. The daily wire count sits above both and will get reconciled at the next WHO publication. The charts treat bars through 28 June as Sitrep-stable, so the 29 June through 7 July bars stay provisional. There is no row for 27 June or 8 July. BNO posted no graphic on 27 June, and Congo's reporting delay means no 8 July figure had been released at the time of this update.
