## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**May 28.** Africa CDC Director-General Jean Kaseya presented [reconciled figures at the agency's daily briefing](https://www.cidrap.umn.edu/ebola/uganda-shuts-dr-congo-border-ebola-outbreak-continues-grow): **DRC 1,077 suspected cases, 129 lab-confirmed, 246 suspected deaths, 18 confirmed deaths**, alongside Uganda's 8 confirmed and 1 death. Kaseya warned that international funding pledges have already dropped from $500 million to $290 million, and called out travel restrictions imposed by some Western countries: "It's not acceptable. We cannot afford to stop this outbreak without resources." Save the Children reports that roughly a quarter of the DRC's confirmed Ebola deaths to date have been children.

WHO Director-General Tedros published a [message to the people of the Democratic Republic of the Congo](https://www.cidrap.umn.edu/ebola/uganda-shuts-dr-congo-border-ebola-outbreak-continues-grow) announcing he will travel to Bunia in the coming days and renewing his call for a ceasefire in Ituri: "No cause, no conflict, no grievance is worth condemning innocent people to death from a preventable disease. A ceasefire, even a temporary one, would save lives." European Union aid (gloves, masks, boots, medication) reached Bunia today via UNICEF, with more deliveries expected over the next eight days. The Guardian reports that several doctors and nurses treating patients in DRC have died after contracting the virus.

The US State Department [allocated an additional $80 million](https://www.cidrap.umn.edu/ebola/uganda-shuts-dr-congo-border-ebola-outbreak-continues-grow) to UNICEF, the World Food Program, World Vision, and the International Organization for Migration to scale up PPE, diagnostics, border screening, and contact tracing, and committed $50 million to UN OCHA for up to 50 Ebola response clinics. Kenya agreed to host a 50-bed US-run quarantine unit for Americans exposed to Ebola, with more than 30 US public health officers deploying to staff it. The Infectious Diseases Society of America publicly criticized the plan, saying the US already has specialized treatment centers and that standing up a high-quality clinic in Kenya during an active outbreak raises serious questions about timing, resources, and standard of care.
