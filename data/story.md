## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**July 14.** The BNO daily graphic, sourced to the health ministries and WHO, puts the outbreak at **2,032 confirmed cases and 756 confirmed deaths**. Global confirmed cases crossed 2,000 for the first time. DRC carries almost all of it, 2,011 cases and 754 deaths, a case fatality ratio near **37.5 percent**. Uganda holds at 20 cases and 2 deaths, no new case since 21 June. France stays at its single imported case, recovered.

This is a running wire count, not a reconciled WHO figure. The gold-standard source is still [Weekly Situation Report 09](https://www.afro.who.int/countries/democratic-republic-of-congo/publication/ebola-bundibugyo-virus-disease-outbreak-2), data as of 12 July, which put the outbreak at 1,984 confirmed and 721 deaths with a 36.6 percent fatality ratio. The two-day gap from the Sitrep to the wire adds 48 cases and 35 deaths. A DRC Ministry of Health figure carried by Times of India matches the BNO count, so the 2,000 crossing has two sources.

The fatality ratio is still climbing while cases climb. That pattern has held for weeks. Bundibugyo has no approved vaccine and no approved treatment, so the number that matters is how fast cases get found and isolated, and the ratio says detection is losing the race. The people being counted are the ones already sick enough to die.

The geography stopped at five provinces this week. WHO's last count was 42 health zones, 33 of them with active transmission, after Haut-Uele and Tshopo were added on 12 July. Tshopo's capital is Kisangani, one of the largest cities in the country. Haut-Uele borders South Sudan and the Central African Republic. CDC has not re-scoped its Level 3 travel notice, which still names only the original three provinces.

Bars through 12 July render solid on the charts. Everything after 12 July stays provisional until Sitrep 10 reconciles it.
