## Story so far

The 2026 Ebola outbreak began in early May in the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Ituri Province. The earliest known symptom onset is April 24; authorities formally declared the outbreak on May 15. Laboratory analysis at the Institut National de Recherche Biomedicale identified the pathogen as **Bundibugyo virus (BDBV)**, one of four orthoebolavirus species that cause Ebola disease in humans. Unlike the more common Zaire ebolavirus, Bundibugyo currently has no approved vaccine or specific therapeutic.

The World Health Organization declared the outbreak a [Public Health Emergency of International Concern](https://www.who.int/news/item/17-05-2026-epidemic-of-ebola-disease-in-the-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-and-uganda-determined-a-public-health-emergency-of-international-concern) on May 17. Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security on May 18. Within the first ten days, cases spread across 12 health zones in three DRC provinces (Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu) plus imported and locally-transmitted cases in Kampala, Uganda.

The response is operating under acute constraints: armed conflict in the affected zones, community distrust of containment measures (multiple Ebola treatment centers have been attacked, with patients escaping), high population mobility, and the absence of a Bundibugyo-specific vaccine. Healthcare-associated infections in Uganda raise concerns about undetected transmission chains in urban centers.

## Latest update

**June 10.** The [BNO News daily graphic for 10 June](https://x.com/BNOFeed/status/2064800984191193477), sourced to the DRC and Uganda health ministries and WHO, puts DRC at **635 confirmed cases and 127 deaths** and Uganda unchanged at **19 confirmed and 2 deaths**. Suspected cases hold at 116. **Global: 654 confirmed, 116 suspected, 129 confirmed deaths.** DRC added 37 confirmed cases and 12 deaths in a day. The newest reconciled WHO figure is still [DON606](https://www.who.int/emergencies/disease-outbreak-news/item/2026-DON606), published 8 June with data as of 6 June: 534 confirmed and 93 deaths across both countries, a 17.4% case fatality rate. The daily wire now runs 120 confirmed cases ahead of that anchor.

Here is the part worth watching. For weeks the assumption baked into this tracker was that the daily wire overshoots WHO, by 15 to 18% each time WHO caught up. DON606 broke that. WHO put DRC at 515 confirmed as of 6 June. The same-day wire had said 488. So WHO came in higher, not lower. The reason is in the report: DRC scaled up testing and is now clearing a backlog of old samples, and a confirmation that lands today can belong to an infection from a week ago. WHO attributes those backlog confirmations to earlier dates, so its reconciled count for 6 June ran ahead of the running wire tally for the same day. I revised the 6 June row up to match WHO. That collapses the old 6-to-7 June jump to flat and means the next reconciliation could move the 7 to 9 June counts in either direction, not just down. The overshoot rule of thumb is no longer safe to lean on.

The structure of the outbreak has not moved. Bundibugyo still has no approved vaccine or treatment, so the ceiling is set by how fast cases get found and isolated. That is the whole mechanism in the CDC model from 5 June: at 20% of cases isolated within two days the outbreak can rival the 2014-2016 West Africa epidemic, and at 70% there is a 94% chance of holding it under 10,000. DON606 reports contact follow-up at 43% in Ituri, the epicenter, well short of the target, though North and South Kivu are above 80%. Ituri alone carries 94% of DRC's confirmed cases. Response operations still run against armed conflict, security incidents at health facilities, and community resistance. WHO again advised against travel and trade restrictions, but the US entry measures the DRC health minister called discriminatory remain in place. WHO Weekly Sitrep 03 (data as of 31 May) is still the latest weekly reconciliation: DRC 321 confirmed, 48 deaths, 15% case fatality. Sitrep 04 has not published. DON606 is now the freshest WHO anchor, and it stops at 6 June, so the 7 to 10 June daily counts still sit ahead of it.
